PROMPT ENGINEERING YOUR WAY TO AN IVY LEAGUE DEGREE: A MODEST PROPOSAL FOR THE DIGITAL AGE

By: Farukh Baykhanov

CHAPTER I: THE BURDEN OF THE HUMAN ELEMENT

I t's kind of sad to see young people, whether at fancy private schools or regular public high schools, weighed down by the tiring and inefficient requirements to just live their lives. Students have to use up a lot of mental energy on three tough, old-fashioned things: keeping up real friendships, going through emotional family dinners, and stressing over how to write a "real" college admissions essay. It's surprising how much time can just get wasted. If a teenager spends two hours comforting a friend through a breakup, they've pretty much lost out on a chance to improve their extracurricular activities. If a kid has a really messy argument with a parent their focus then wanders from the goal of getting into a prestigious school to something else.

The ways we try to handle these problems now like getting no sleep, feeling super stressed, or going to expensive therapy that doesn't get covered by insurance, just don't cut it. They only deal with the problems, but not the actual cause of them. And the cause, simply put, is being human.

We're in a time where AI can write perfect code, predict how economies work, and even copy old art styles. Yet our kids are still struggling through the messy business of being human.

So, here's my modest proposal: let's just automate everything about being a teenager. If we let computer programs take care of all people's social life, family duties, and creative pursuits we can finally get rid of all the headache that comes with being a person. This would make teenagers’ road to an Ivy League school super smooth.

CHAPTER II: THE THREE PILLARS OF LIFE SYNTHESIS

To really automate a person, we have to break down them into 3 core areas. Each area would have an exact, mechanical system that turns all the messy parts of real life into neat, useful information.

1. Admissions Engineering (The Trauma-Plot Generator)

Top universities today want a very specific kind of story: one where a big personal tragedy turns into a success that shows leadership. To write this by hand, you either need to have actually suffered, or at least spend a lot of boring time thinking about yourself.

Our special computer program looks at what your dream Ivy League school wants and matches it with a huge collection of tragic stories. We can take a regular, comfortable suburban background and turn it into a dramatic story about overcoming something like "systemic existential boredom."

For example, you might put in details like "upper-middle class, played lacrosse, broke wrist junior year." The system would then spit out a really moving essay that uses the broken wrist as a way to talk about something bigger, like the shaky economic system of the Western world.

The result? An essay that gets a super high score for how emotionally impactful it is, all without the student feeling any actual pain or stress. The applicant stays a blank slate, totally ready to soak up the culture of a place like Yale or Harvard.

2. Peer-to-Peer Optimization (Auto-Friendship Maintenance)

To truly be seen as important, you need to look popular. But real friendships take up a huge amount of time. Friends need you to be there, listen actively, and share your feelings—none of which really help with college applications.

The answer is to use an Auto-Friendship "wrapper." By linking your personal messaging apps to a local AI that's learned how teenagers talk, the system can manage all your friends on its own.

Scenario: A peer texts you at 11:30 PM: "I'm having a total existential crisis about my future. I don't know who I am."

What a human would actually do (not ideal): You'd stay up for two hours, give real advice, get tired from caring too much, and mess up your sleep schedule right before your AP Chemistry exam.

The computer's solution (the best way): The system spots the sign of emotional distress ("friend in crisis") and immediately sends a perfectly crafted reply: "Wow, that is literally so valid, I am always here for you! You are a literal rockstar. Sending so much love. 💖"

Your friend feels heard, your online popularity numbers go up, and you still get your full eight hours of sleep. Human contact just becomes a bunch of quick, automated messages.

3. The Filial Piety Protocol (The "Good Child" Automation Suite)

Parents are probably the biggest hurdle to doing well in school. They often want to know how you're really feeling, get updates on your mental health, and expect you to be at the dinner table.

To deal with this, we're introducing the Voice-Cloned "Good Child" system. It works through smart speakers and phones, catching questions from parents and sending back pre-recorded, computer-generated answers that sound obedient.

Parental Input: "Are you studying hard? We just want you to be happy."

System Response: "Dear parents, your worry is understood and makes sense. My brain is working perfectly, totally ready for an Ivy League school. I love you in a way that can be measured."

If you actually have to be there in person, the student just puts on noise-canceling headphones playing lecture notes over and over. They'd also wear a simple device that makes their face show a vague, obedient "yes" look throughout the meal.

CHAPTER III: A LITERARY PARALLEL

Of course, some people will criticize this system. People who argue about morals will say that this idea takes away kids' important growing-up years, that it creates a generation of empty, emotionless robots, and that it destroys the whole idea of a human soul.

To these critics, I can only point to the ever-true way of thinking of Dean Jonathan Swift.

Back in 1729, when Ireland had a huge problem with poverty and starvation, Swift realized that emotional solutions just didn't work. He came up with a cold, mathematical, and food-related solution: using poor children to feed the wealthy. It was a perfect supply and demand calculation, completely without all the messy, inefficient talk about right and wrong that had stopped progress for ages.

Our problem today is different on the surface, but it's the same at its core. Today's teenagers are starving—not for food, but for status. The way things are now, they have to give up their time, their sanity, and their true selves just to please the college admissions people.

Wouldn't it be much kinder, then, to just let a machine make that sacrifice for them?

Here's how Swift's idea from 1729 compares to my idea from 2026: Swift's plan solved money problems, used people as a resource, worked in the kitchen, and aimed to feed the rich. My plan solves the problem of inefficient existence, uses human thoughts and feelings as a resource, works with AI, and aims to make the elite feel good.

By letting the algorithm take away the burden of being a person, the student is completely free to become what a top university actually wants: a perfectly refined, high-achieving, data-focused being. After all, real status means getting rid of who you really are.

CHAPTER IV: THE ULTIMATE ADVANTAGE

Let's look at what you gain in the end. By using the Swift-Framework™, today's college applicant gets a huge strategic advantage:

  • Extracurriculars are easier to scale: Without having to spend time eating, talking, or feeling, one student can easily run twelve different high school clubs at the same time.
  • Perfect Emotional Immunity: Rejection letters don't hurt at all, because the "person" getting rejected is just a fake personality made by a bunch of computers in Northern Virginia.
  • Institutional Harmony: Universities get students who are totally predictable, easy to manage, and already set up to follow corporate rules.

I want to be clear: this system isn't for people who are easily scared, or for anyone who still has an old-fashioned liking for things like "joy," "random memories," or "real love." Those things are expensive, unreliable, and you can't get any school credit for them.

Let the algorithms have your youth. They know what to do with it far better than you do.

10 Late 2022

The Initial Spark

November 2022: OpenAI releases ChatGPT. Powered by a fine-tuned version of GPT-3.5, specifically GPT-3.5 turbo, it marks the first time that the broad public has access to frontier large language models. This introduction of generative artificial intelligence changes the human-machine interaction paradigm, kicking off a hyper-accelerated AI arms race in Silicon Valley. This event is the “Netscape moment” of the artificial intelligence industry.

2022
11 2023

The Great Scaling and Multimodal Wars

Early 2023: Big tech scrambles to respond. Microsoft launches a major partnership with OpenAI, integrating GPT models throughout the Office suite of applications. Google deploys its experimental conversational model, Bard. Early adoption of ChatGPT makes it a sensation around the internet.

March 2023: OpenAI releases GPT-4, a model characterized by being a massive leap in reasoning, problem-solving, and standardized test performance.

July 2023: Meta enters the AI race with Llama 2, open-sourcing its weights and igniting a global developer movement to build, fine-tune, and run local models, removing the reliance on proprietary APIs.

2023
12 2024

Multimodal Breakthroughs & The Dawn of Agency

Early to Mid 2024: AI transitions from pure text interfaces such as the common chatbot to multimodal systems. Google introduces the Gemini 1.5 Pro model, boasting a groundbreaking context window of 2 million tokens. OpenAI releases GPT-4o, the “o” representing “omni.” The model introduced voice, text, and image processing natively within the model at high speeds.

Late 2024: The industry shifts focus from "stateless" chat interfaces to "stateful" agentic workflows. OpenAI introduces its o-series reasoning models, demonstrating "System 2" reflective thinking, allowing models to deliberate and self-correct before responding.

2024
13 2025

Market Disruptions & The Agentic Inflection

January 2025: The geopolitical landscape of AI shifts dramatically as Chinese startup DeepSeek releases highly efficient, low-cost open-weights models. The sudden release of frontier-class capabilities in an open-weight model triggers a historic tech-stock sell-off, erasing hundreds of billions of dollars in market cap from hardware giants. This event was known as the “Deepseek moment.”

Mid-to-Late 2025: Agentic AI moves into active production. Anthropic previews the groundbreaking Claude Code, a terminal-based agentic coding tool, transforming the AI from an assistant into an autonomous teammate. OpenAI launches GPT-5, pushing the boundary of complex, multi-step problem solving.

2025
14 2026

The Agentic Frontier

Present Day: Chatbots are largely a relic of the past, or more accurately, only used by people who are not on the frontier of AI. The industry is defined by autonomous agentic systems capable of executing extended, hours-long tasks, from complex software engineering and continuous scientific research to managing entire business pipelines with minimal human intervention.
